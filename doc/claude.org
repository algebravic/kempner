** Setting Up the Fourier Series
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: setting-up-the-fourier-series
:END:
*Notation.* Let \(s_k = \alpha + 2\pi i k\) where
\(\alpha = \log_{10} 11 \approx 1.0414 > 1\). The proposed coefficients
are \[a_k = \frac{\zeta(s_k)}{s_k}.\]

Since \(\operatorname{Re}(s_k) = \alpha > 1\), the Dirichlet series for
\(\zeta\) converges absolutely on this vertical line, and \(a_k\) is
well-defined for all \(k \in \mathbb{Z}\).

--------------

** Identifying the Underlying Function
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: identifying-the-underlying-function
:END:
A classical integral representation gives, for
\(\operatorname{Re}(s)>1\):
\[\frac{\zeta(s)}{s} = \int_1^\infty \lfloor t \rfloor\, t^{-s-1}\,dt.\]

Substituting \(t = e^u\) (so \(dt = e^u\,du\)):
\[a_k = \int_0^\infty \underbrace{\lfloor e^u\rfloor e^{-\alpha u}}_{g(u)}\, e^{-2\pi i k u}\, du = \hat{g}(k),\]

i.e. \(a_k\) is the *Fourier transform of*
\(g(u) = \lfloor e^u\rfloor e^{-\alpha u}\) *evaluated at the integers*.
By the standard periodisation identity, the Fourier series has
coefficients equal to those of the period-1 function
\[\boxed{h(x) = \sum_{n=0}^\infty \lfloor e^{x+n}\rfloor\, e^{-\alpha(x+n)}, \quad x\in[0,1),}\]
extended periodically. (The sum converges since each term
\(\sim e^{(1-\alpha)n}\to 0\).)

--------------

** Bounded Variation --- the Key Structural Fact
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: bounded-variation-the-key-structural-fact
:END:
The function \(h\) is *piecewise smooth between jumps*. The jumps arise
exactly when \(e^{x+n}\) crosses an integer \(m\), i.e. at the points
\[x_m = \{\ln m\} = \ln m - \lfloor\ln m\rfloor, \quad m = 2, 3, 4, \ldots\]

At each such point the floor function increases by 1, so the jump size
is \[\Delta h(x_m) = e^{-\alpha \ln m} = m^{-\alpha}.\]

The *total variation* is therefore
\[\operatorname{TV}(h) \;\le\; \underbrace{(1 - e^{-\alpha})}_{\text{smooth part}} + \underbrace{\sum_{m=2}^\infty m^{-\alpha}}_{\text{jump part}} = (1-e^{-\alpha}) + \bigl(\zeta(\alpha)-1\bigr) < \infty,\]

since \(\alpha > 1\) ensures \(\zeta(\alpha)<\infty\). Thus
\(h\in \mathrm{BV}[0,1]\).

--------------

** Convergence Result
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: convergence-result
:END:
By the *Dirichlet--Jordan theorem*: if \(f\in \mathrm{BV}[0,1]\), its
Fourier series converges pointwise *at every* \(x\) to the symmetric
average \(\tfrac{1}{2}(f(x^+)+f(x^-))\).

*The Fourier series therefore converges for all \(x\in\mathbb{R}\)*,
with limit:

| \(x \mod 1\)                                 | Limit of Fourier series                                       |
|----------------------------------------------+---------------------------------------------------------------|
| \(\ne \{\ln m\}\) for any integer \(m\ge 2\) | \(h(x)\) (continuity point)                                   |
| \(= \{\ln m\}\) for some integer \(m\ge 2\)  | \(\dfrac{h(x^+)+h(x^-)}{2} = h(x) - \dfrac{1}{2}m^{-\alpha}\) |

The *exceptional set* \(\mathcal{E} = \{\ln m \bmod 1 : m\ge 2\}\) is
/dense/ in \([0,1)\) (since \(\ln(m+1)-\ln m\to 0\)), yet it is only
countable, so the Fourier series still converges *everywhere* --- to
\(h(x)\) on the complement of \(\mathcal{E}\), and to the midpoint of
the jump on \(\mathcal{E}\).

--------------

** Summary
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: summary
:END:
The specific choice \(\alpha = \log_{10}11\) matters in exactly one way:
it places \(s_k\) strictly inside the half-plane
\(\operatorname{Re}(s)>1\), guaranteeing \(\zeta(\alpha)<\infty\) and
hence \(\operatorname{TV}(h)<\infty\). Any \(\alpha>1\) would give the
same conclusion. The Fourier series *converges pointwise at every real
argument*.
